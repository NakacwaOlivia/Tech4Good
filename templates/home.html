GET TO KNOW THE DIFFERENT SOIL TYPES AND THEIR IMPORTANCES

Chalk Soil

Chalky soils contain a high amount of calcium carbonate, making them high-alkaline soils. Chalky soil has a fine soil texture that dries out easily. Plants that require acidic soil will not survive in chalky soil. In some cases, chalky soil can be acidified but more often than not, gardeners are better off only using plants that tolerate alkaline soils.

Clay Soil

They are dense, high in nutrients, and contain more than twenty-five percent clay particles. Clay soil particles are small and tightly packed together with very little airspace between particles. They retain a high amount of water but offer poor drainage. The size of the particles makes it difficult for plant roots to take hold and can prove to be a challenge.

Loam Soil

Loamy soils contain a mix of clay, sand, silt, and humus. Depending on the ratio of the different soil types that make up loam soil, it may be considered clay-loam, loamy sand, etc. Loamy soils are high in nutrients, have good water retention, and have good drainage. It is one of the best agricultural soils and is ideal for use in a raised garden bed.

Sand Soil

Sandy soils contain a high concentration of rock particles. They are light, warm, and airy soils that tend to have an acidic pH level. They are free-draining soils that offer very little in the way of nutrients and water retention, which can hinder plant growth. The addition of organic matter can improve the soil structure, providing plant roots with more nutrients and water.

Peat Soil

Peat soils are high in organic material. They are high in nutrients and provide very good drainage. Peat soils are typically added to existing topsoil to amend the soil structure and provide nutrients for plant growth.

Silt Soil

Silty soils have good aeration and a high fertility rating. Silt particles are smaller than sand particles but larger than clay particles. They retain a high amount of moisture but can be easily washed away by heavy rains or currents.

MAIN CAUSES OF SOIL INFERTILITY

Acidity levels

Plants grow because they feed off of the nutrients that they receive from the soil. The acidity level of the soil has a direct impact on the ability of plants to absorb these nutrients. As the acidity level of the soil rises, the amount of energy required by plants to absorb key nutrients increases, making it more difficult for the plants to absorb them. When soil is too acidic, plants cannot absorb the nutrients they need, thus rendering the soil infertile and unable to produce growth.

Nutritional Composition

The nutritional composition of your soil also has a direct impact on its fertility. Plants use three basic elements from the soil to grow. These elements are nitrogen, potassium, and phosphorus. If your soil lacks the appropriate balance of these elements, plants will not have enough nutrients to grow, resulting in soil infertility. Plants also require iron, zinc, magnesium, and some other elements for growth, so it’s also important that your soil has the correct amounts of these nutrients.

Poor Soil Structure

The way your soil is made up can affect how plant roots access the water and oxygen needed to draw out nutrients from the soil. The structure of the soil impacts how much water and oxygen are available for plant roots. If your soil is made up of large particles, this provides enough room for oxygen but results in the over-drainage of water. On the other hand, if your soil is made up of smaller particles, this prevents water from draining and oxygen from flowing. Finding the right balance is key.

Salinity and Sodicity

If there are high levels of salt in your soil, this will prevent plants and grass from absorbing water. This will also eventually ruin the physical structure of your soil. Soil with high salinity levels is often found in areas with natural salt deposits. Using certain chemicals and synthetic fertilizers have also been shown to increase soil salinity, causing the soil to become infertile.Sodic soils occur when there is too much sodium in the soil itself. These soils will often have decreased levels of calcium and magnesium, increased pH levels between 8.5 and 10.0 and degraded soil structure.

Soil PH Levels

Soil fertility problems may also occur when the soil pH is outside the ideal range for plant growth. Most plants can grow when the soil pH is between 5.5 and 7.0. Some plants, such as azaleas and blueberries, will do well in more acidic soils with a pH under 5.5. Acidic soils affect soil fertility by decreasing the availability of critical nutrients, such as magnesium, calcium and potassium, while increasing the availability of elements that can become toxic in large amounts, such as iron, zinc and aluminum. Correct acidic soil by adding lime and mixing it into the soil in the amount recommended by your soil test results.

Soil Erosion

Erosion is another of the factors affecting soil fertility. This is a common concern, especially when you are growing crops on a slope, notes Iowa State University Extension and Outreach. As the topsoil erodes, the soil nutrients decrease, and soil quality and structure decline. The soil is also limited in its ability to hold water. With limited topsoil, plants reach down into the subsoil. In many cases, the subsoil is inhospitable to plant roots. Coarse subsoil that consists of gravel and sand particles will not hold enough water to support the plants. Subsoil that has fine particles prevents water drainage and limits oxygen availability to the roots.
